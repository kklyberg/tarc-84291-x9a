RF Link Budget & Horizon Bounds

1.0 SURFACE CURVATURE CONSTRAINTS (LOS): Commercial two-way radio frequencies propagate via straight-line geometric paths (Line-of-Sight). Consumer marketing materials often advertise "30-mile handheld ranges," which violates basic earth geometry. Handheld nodes operating at typical ground heights (h = 5 ft) encounter a physical horizon limitation at ~3.1 miles. Transmissions extending beyond this point overshoot the curvature of the earth and dissipate into space.

1.1 Obstruction & Path Attenuation Log

Linear RF power (Wattage) cannot bend around solid geographical or structural barriers. Dense pine canopy, concrete urban grids, and corrugated steel blocks act as heavy attenuation filters. A 5W transceiver achieving 10+ miles over unobstructed salt water will experience a link budget collapse to < 0.8 miles inside heavy commercial concrete infrastructure.

2.0 Calculation Methodology

  1. Band Selection: VHF provides superior knife-edge diffraction around foliage; UHF cuts through dense structural concrete walls; 800MHz is optimized for trunked public safety grids.
  2. Elevation Mapping: Adjust the TX/RX sliders to lengthen the geometric radio horizon limit via the formula: d = √(2×h1) + √(2×h2).
  3. Clutter Profile Selection: Applies the Log-Distance Path Loss exponent matrix against free-space attenuation baselines.
  4. Infrastructure Link: Toggles a middle-bridge repeater node to establish independent dual-path link calculations (Leg A + Leg B).

Engineering Axiom

When dealing with negative link margins, increasing terminal wattage power outputs provides diminishing returns. Antenna radiation height is the primary system vector. Raising a structural mast by 20 feet recovers more decibel margin than quadrupling terminal amplifier wattage.
