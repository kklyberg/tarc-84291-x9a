Wireless Engineering Guide

The Truth About Two-Way Range

You have likely seen retail radios advertised boasting a "30 to 40-Mile Range". In the real world, this is physically impossible. Radio waves travel in straight lines (Line-of-Sight). Because the Earth curves roughly every 3 to 5 miles, a signal transmitted from a handheld radio at ground level will shoot straight off into space rather than following the ground.

The True Enemy: RF Clutter

Power (Wattage) does not bend around solid obstacles. Steel frames, concrete city walls, high-density pine foliage, and terrain hills aggressively soak up or bounce back radio frequencies. A 5-Watt commercial radio that punches 15 miles over open water might only travel less than 1 mile deep inside an urban commercial center.

How to Use This Simulator

  1. Select Your Band: VHF bends around rural foliage better; UHF cuts through concrete walls; 800MHz is optimized for public safety.
  2. Set Node Heights: Physically raise your transmitter and receiver heights to watch the geometric horizon extend.
  3. Choose Environment Profile: Watch how your usable range instantly collapses the moment you switch from Open Flat Grounds to Urban Clutter.
  4. Deploy a Repeater: Check the box to simulate placing an elevated infrastructure tower between nodes to recover lost boundaries.

T.A. Radio Pro Tip:

If your calculated link margin falls into the negative, adding more power rarely solves the issue. Elevation is King. Raising your tower or antenna placement by 20 feet is worth more than quadrupling your radio transmitter wattage. Try adjusting the height sliders on the right to see the math in action!
